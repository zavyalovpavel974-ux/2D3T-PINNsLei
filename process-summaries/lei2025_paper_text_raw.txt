The 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations can approximate the propagation of energy in the material, as well
as the energy exchange process of electrons, ions and photons. In many cases, the computational time to
solve these equations accounts for a large proportion (more than 80%) of that of the entire simulation of
radiation hydrodynamics. PINNs is a promising way to solve partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs). Although
numerical methods have been successful in solving 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations, the PINNs method also
has some advantages, such as mesh-free, suitable to high dimension and complex domain problems. But the
original PINNs cannot solve the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations to a reasonable precision. This work aims to
explore which techniques need to be added to PINNs and to what extent it can address the challenges posed by
strong nonlinearity and multi-scale phenomena present in 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations. Hence, we adopt
guaranteed positive constraint to the outputs so that the network can be trained, give a relatively large weight to
the initial loss and Dirichlet boundary loss, take the logarithm of the initial loss, use transfer learning and Fourier
feature embedding to improve accuracy. We call our improved approach 2D 3T PINNs. Numerical experiments
show that the relative 𝐿2 and the absolute 𝐿∞ error between the 2D 3T PINNs prediction and the reference
solution is of the order of 10−2

1. Introduction
The 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations are a model equation in the
numerical simulation of inertial co­finement fusion (ICF) [1,2]. Its back­
ground is the energy equation in the radiation hydrodynamic equations,
which approximately describes the propagation of radiation energy in
a stationary medium, and the energy exchange process between elec­
trons, ions and photons. Since the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations
has the characteristics of strong nonlinearity, multi-scale [3,4], strong
discontinuity (multi-material), and existence of sudden change regions,
it brings many diﬃculties to the discretization (finite volume method
is commonly used) [5--8] of the equation and the fast algorithm design
(fast algorithms for algebraic equations) [9--12] of the discrete system.
In many cases, the calculation time for solving the energy equation ac­
counts for a large proportion (more than 80%) of the entire simulation
process. Although there are many diﬃculties in solving the 2-D 3-T heat
conduction equations, the numerical method has achieved successful

Because of the universal approximation theorem [13] of deep neural
networks, it can be used to accurately approximate any continuous func­
tion. In 1990, Lee et al. used neural networks for the first time to solve
partial diﬀerential equations [14]. In 1998, on the basis of previous re­
search, Lagaris et al. [15] further proposed a method to solve ODE and
PDE using neural network. However, the practical application of these
methods is greatly limited due to the insuﬃcient computing power of
early devices and the complexity of constructing neural networks. In
2019, Raissi et al. utilize GPU devices with powerful computing capabil­
ities and high-performance and easy-to-use deep learning frameworks,
such as Pytorch [16], Tenso­flow [17] and PaddlePaddle [18]. They
proposed Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) [19] to solve PDEs.
Deep learning methods for solving PDEs have returned to people’s vision
and have received extensive attention after that time.
In recent years, there have been a lot of work on solving PDE based
on PINNs. For example, Laubscher [20] applies PINNs to predict momen­

the fluid flow and heat transfer problems. Jalili et al. [29] utilize PINNs
for simulations of two-phase fluid processes with heat transfer.
PINNs have been shown to have powerful approximation capabil­
ities for some PDEs. On the one hand, due to the importance of the
2-D 3-T heat conduction equations and the progress made by PINNs on
some PDEs. On the other hand, previous work has shown that PINNs is
easier to solve linear PDE, or the solution is smooth, and the solution
is low-frequency PDE. PINNs is diﬃcult to solve the strong nonlinear
PDE, the solution is multi-scale or discontinuous. There is little work
to solve these problems with PINNs, and the 2-D 3-T heat conduction
equations has strong nonlinearity, multi-scale and discontinuity at the
same time. Therefore, we would like to try to use PINNs to solve the
2-D 3-T heat conduction equations. However, we find that it cannot
be solved using the original PINNs, our analysis suggests that the com­
plexity of the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations and the coupling of
several diﬃculties are the main obstacles. Therefore, we simpl­fied the
equation parameters and gradually decoupled the challenges, proposing
eﬀective technologies for each diﬃculty. As the equation parameters be­
came more complex and the challenges increased, we continued to add
new technologies on top of those already implemented to address more
complex situations. In view of the characteristics and diﬃculties of 2-D
3-T heat conduction equations, we add some new techniques based on
PINNs. We call our method 2D 3T PINNs. For details, see Section 3.2.
In the case of multiple materials, the solution of 2-D 3-T heat con­
duction equations is discontinuous, and this problem is considered as
future work. In this article, we try to use PINNs to explore the two
aspects of strong nonlinear and multi-scale problems of 2-D 3-T heat
conduction equations, and to see how diﬃcult it can be solved, to ex­
plore the parameter range in which PINNs can successfully solve the 2-D
3-T heat conduction equations. In the process of exploring what tech­
nology should be used to solve the diﬃculties is eﬀective, and to what
extent can the diﬃculties be solved.
• We propose a novel algorithm based on PINNs to solve the 2-D
3-T heat conduction equations. To the best of our knowledge, we
are the first to try using neural networks to solve the 2-D 3-T heat
conduction equations.
• We find that with slight changes in constructing the exact solution,
the solving of PINNs behaves very diﬀerently.
• 2D 3T PINNs mainly uses five techniques, adding guaranteed pos­
itive constraints to the network output, giving a relatively large
weight to the initial loss and Dirichlet boundary loss, taking the
logarithm of the initial loss, transfer learning and Fourier feature
embedding.
• We present a series of numerical experiments to demonstrate the
accuracy and eﬃciency of the proposed algorithm.

3.2. 2D 3T PINNs
We analyzed the characteristics and diﬃculties of 2-D 3-T heat con­
duction equations and proposed five eﬀective techniques. We started
with simpl­fied equations, ver­fied the corresponding techniques, and
then gradually made the equations more complex, adding new tech­
niques on top of the previous techniques to solve the newly added
diﬃculties. First, the temperature in the equation must be positive, so
we added a positive constraint to the network output. Secondly, we an­
alyzed the boundary conditions, because only one edge has a Dirichlet
boundary condition for photons, and the other edges have Newman
boundary conditions. The diﬀusion and exchange of temperature are
caused by the Dirichlet boundary condition, so it should be given the
largest weight. We proposed a weight strategy suitable for the 2-D 3­
T heat conduction equations. Then, we analyzed the initial conditions
and found that the initial temperature was very low. In order to make
the network learn the initial conditions well, we took the logarithm of
the initial condition loss. Then, we analyzed the diﬃculties in the equa­
tion. The equation has multi-scale diﬃculties, mainly due to 𝐴𝑒𝑖, so we
ﬁrst solve the equation with a smaller 𝐴𝑒𝑖, and then solve the equa­
tion with a larger 𝐴𝑒𝑖. In order to solve this diﬃculty, we introduced
transfer learning. The equation also has strong nonlinear diﬃculties. In
order to enable the network to capture high-frequency information, we
introduced Fourier feature embedding and added some Fourier feature
inputs. In this subsection, we introduce the introduced techniques in
detail for each feature or diﬃculty and combine them with the original
PINN to increase the expressive power of the original PINN. We refer to
our proposed new algorithm as 2D 3T PINNs. Fig. 2 is the architecture
of our 2D 3T PINNs.

3.2.1. Guaranteed positive constraint
PINNs directly solve the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations, and NaN
will appear during the training process, making the training unable to
continue. The reason is that the temperature calculated by PINNs will
appear negative, and the equation needs to be squared to the temper­
ature, so NaN will appear. To address this issue, we propose the first
improved strategy to PINNs. We put a guaranteed positive constraint
on the output of the network, that is, 𝑙𝑛(1 + 𝑒𝑢(𝑡,𝐱;𝜃)). After adding guar­
anteed positive constraint to PINNs, PINNs can train, but there is no
accuracy in solving 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations. To solve the
problem that the guaranteed positive PINNs cannot be trained to a rea­
sonable precision, we propose a weighting strategy.

3.2.4. Transfer learning
Transfer learning is a technique worth trying when the target task
is similar to the source task. It is mainly divided into two stages. The
ﬁrst stage is the pre-training stage, which is to train on a source task
with a large amount of data to obtain a good set of initial parameters.
The second stage is the model fine-tuning stage, which is to fine-tune
the model parameters on the target task. In [32], the author calls it
curriculum PINN regularization, which is used to solve 1D convection
equations with diﬀerent 𝛽. In the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations, we
found that as 𝐴𝑒𝑖 increases from 70 to 400, 2D 3T PINNs will encounter
diﬃculties in solving, therefore, we combined transfer learning to suc­
cessfully handle 𝐴𝑒𝑖 up to 400. Where we diﬀer from [32] is that we
reduce Adam’s initial learning rate by an order of magnitude during the
ﬁne-tuning phase. That is, when training 𝐴𝑒𝑖 = 70, Adam’s initial learn­
ing rate is 1×10−3, when training 𝐴𝑒𝑖 = 400, in order to just fine-tune on
the trained network, Adam’s initial learning rate is reduced to 1 × 10−4.
3.2.5. Fourier feature embedding
Fourier features network [33] can help alleviate the challenges as­
sociated with spectral bias, allowing neural networks to better capture
and learn high-frequency information. We add some random Fourier
features [34] cos(𝐁𝐱) and sin(𝐁𝐱) to the input, where 𝐁 ∈ ℝ𝑚×𝑑 each
element is sampled from Gaussian distribution  (0,1), in our ex­
periment, 𝑚 = 1000, generally, the larger 𝑚 is, the better the net­
work trains, but the computational cost is higher. The diﬀerence be­
tween us and [34] is that our input is (𝑡,𝑥,𝑦,cos(𝐁𝐱),sin(𝐁𝐱)), not
(cos(𝐁𝐱),sin(𝐁𝐱),cos(𝐁𝑡),sin(𝐁𝑡)).
In summary, we use five techniques to improve the approximation
capabilities of PINNs.
• The network output has added a positive-preserving constraint.
• The photon Dirichlet boundary condition gives the maximum
weight, followed by the initial value weight.
• Logarithm of initial loss.
• Transfer learning.
• Fourier feature embedding.

5.6. Example 6: data-driven discovery of partial diﬀerential equations
In this example, we use 2D 3T PINNs to solve the inverse prob­
lem of the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations. Suppose we have some
data points, but the density 𝜌 is unknown in the equations. We use the
equation parameter setting of Example 2. Diﬀerent from Example 2, we
added reference solutions at five moments, 1 × 10−5, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, and
1, as supervision data in the training data of 2D 3T PINNs, taking 100
points at each moment, and taking the density 𝜌 as a trainable parame­
ter of the network. We added five moments of supervised losses to the
loss function, all with a weight of 1000, and the weights of the remain­
ing losses are the same as in Example 2.
Table 12 shows the density values predicted by 2D 3T PINNs when
using 0.5 and 1 as the initial value respectively. The relative errors be­
tween them and the true values are 1.579% and 1.561% respectively. It
can be seen that the 2D 3T PINNs inversion density has achieved the
desired accuracy. Fig. 11 shows the predicted values at time 𝑡 = 1. It
can be seen that 2D 3T PINNs not only successfully inverts the density
value, but the solution predicted by the trained network at time 𝑡 = 1
also achieves the expected accuracy.
6. Conclusions and future work
We explore using PINNs to solve the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equa­
tions with strong nonlinearity and multi-scale. Even for 2-D 3-T heat

conduction equations with simple parameters, PINNs cannot achieve
the desired accuracy. According to the diﬃculties encountered in the
process of solving, we combine five techniques - positive constraint,
weighted strategy, logarithmic initial value loss, transfer learning and
Fourier feature embedding, and propose our new method 2D 3T PINNs.
The most complex parameter range that 2D 3T PINNs can solve at
present is 𝐾𝑟 = 𝐴𝑟𝑇𝑟, 𝐴𝑒𝑖 ≤ 700, and the remaining parameters are prac­
tical application parameters. The relative 𝐿2-norm error and 𝐿∞-norm
error are on the order of 10−2 − 10−3 in the case where it is currently
solvable.
From our experiments, we summarized several experiences: For the
output to be non-negative, positivity constraints are necessary; Weights
are very important, good weights can eﬀectively reduce multiple losses,
and the initial boundary value weight should be given a relatively large
weight; When the initial value condition is very small, logarithmic initial
value loss can make the loss of initial conditions smaller, thereby making
the solution prediction more accurate at the initial moment; As can be
seen from Example 3, when a parameter in the PDE changes within a
certain range (the range is related to the problem), that is, the equation
multi-scale enhancement, transfer learning is eﬀective; As can be seen
from Example 4, Fourier feature embedding is helpful to the nonlinear
enhancement of equations and multi-scale enhancement; As can be seen
from Example 5, for diﬃcult problems with stronger nonlinearity and
stronger multi-scale, combinatorial techniques are necessary.
For the future work, we hope to successfully solve for 𝐾𝑟 = 𝐴𝑟𝑇 3+𝛽
𝑟
and 𝐴𝑒𝑖 = 7000. We have also tried several methods of adaptive weight,
but have not achieved good results so far. We will continue to ex­
plore in the future, hoping to use adaptive methods to balance multiple
losses. We plan to use PINNs to provide initial values for the non-exact
Newton method for the 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations. For the
multi-material 2-D 3-T heat conduction equations, we plan to use PINNs
combined with domain decomposition to solve, on the one hand, it may
be more accurate, on the other hand, it can be accelerated. We are also
going to use PINNs to solve the 3-D 3-T heat conduction equations.
